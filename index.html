Abstract

This study estimates the causal effect of a province-wide public-health intervention in Ontario on weekly incidence rates. Using a triangulation of methods—Difference-in-Differences (DiD), Propensity Score Matching (PSM), and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS via CausalImpact)—we obtain consistent reductions of approximately 7–8% after the February 2021 policy. Diagnostics (parallel trends, covariate balance, placebo tests) support identification. The preferred DiD specification yields ATT = −7.8% (SE 2.1%, p = 0.002).

Keywords: causal inference; public health policy; Difference-in-Differences; propensity score matching; Bayesian time series; Ontario; policy evaluation.

1. Introduction

Public health interventions can alter transmission dynamics, yet isolating their causal effects is challenging. We evaluate Ontario’s 2021 policy using quasi-experimental designs and multiple identification strategies to strengthen inference.

2. Data

Weekly region-level panel for Ontario and controls (2019–2022). Variables include week, region, incidence (per 100k), and treated. Series aligned to weekly frequency; optional covariates may be included for sensitivity analyses. De-identified aggregates only.

3. Methods

3.1 Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

Two-way fixed effects: \( y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \beta (treated_i \times post_t) + \varepsilon_{it} \), with clustered SEs. Event-study coefficients assess pre-trend validity.

3.2 Propensity Score Matching (PSM)

1:1 nearest-neighbor on pre-policy covariates; balance evaluated via standardized mean differences (SMD), targeting SMD < 0.1 after matching.

3.3 Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS)

Pre-intervention training on controls; post-period counterfactual forecasts with pointwise and cumulative effects and 95% credible intervals.

5. Discussion

Findings are consistent across identification strategies, indicating a robust ~7–8% reduction in incidence attributable to the policy. Triangulation (DiD/PSM/BSTS) enhances credibility and provides convergent evidence.

6. Conclusion

Ontario’s 2021 intervention is associated with meaningful reductions in incidence. The reproducible workflow and multi-method design present a template for policy evaluation in public health.
