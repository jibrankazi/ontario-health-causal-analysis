Abstract

This study evaluates the causal effect of the 2021 Ontario Public Health Intervention (O-PHI) on regional incidence rates using **methodological triangulation**. We employed Difference-in-Differences (DiD), Propensity Score Matching (PSM), and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS) to derive a robust estimate of the Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT). The analysis resulted in **critical non-convergence** across estimates: the DiD model indicated a non-significant reduction of **$-108.6$ units** ($p=0.30$), while the BSTS counterfactual suggested a significant **$+86.5\%$ increase** in incidence relative to the synthetic control. Diagnostics, including event-study tests, support the internal validity of the DiD structure, suggesting the observed discrepancy is due to the inherent instability or differential assumptions about the counterfactual. These conflicting findings necessitate caution in policy conclusions and underscore the need for advanced causal methods, such as Synthetic Difference-in-Differences (SDID) and Causal Forests, to achieve convergence.

Keywords: Causal Inference; DiD; BSTS; Non-Convergence; Policy Evaluation; Incidence Rates.

1. Introduction

The accurate estimation of public health policy impact in non-randomized settings requires rigorous quasi-experimental methods. This report summarizes the findings from the first phase of an analysis dedicated to assessing the causal effect of the O-PHI on weekly incidence rates in Ontario compared to a selected control group of provinces/regions. Our initial strategy relied on triangulating three distinct causal estimators to ensure confidence in the final ATT. The subsequent sections detail the methods employed and the unexpected empirical results.

2. Methodology

The analysis uses a weekly panel dataset spanning 2019–2022. The identification strategy hinges on the construction of a valid counterfactual, employing three distinct techniques, each with unique identifying assumptions:

  • **Difference-in-Differences (DiD):** Relies on the **parallel trends assumption**, formally tested via an event-study model. Standard errors are clustered at the region level.
  • **Propensity Score Matching (PSM):** Used to construct a covariate-balanced control group based on pre-intervention characteristics, mitigating selection bias.
  • **Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS):** Generates a data-driven synthetic counterfactual, providing a posterior probability of a causal effect by comparing the observed outcome to the forecasted (untreated) outcome.

3. Empirical Results

Diagnostic checks confirmed the suitability of the data for each model. Specifically, the DiD event-study plot showed statistically insignificant pre-trends, satisfying the core parallel trends assumption. PSM achieved high post-match balance (SMD $<0.1$). 4. Discussion and Path Forward

The primary finding is a fundamental methodological non-convergence. While both traditional DiD and PSM point towards a policy-induced reduction (albeit non-significant for DiD), the sophisticated BSTS model strongly rejects this conclusion, indicating a significant rise in incidence relative to the synthetic baseline. This contradiction invalidates the presentation of a single, unified ATT.

This outcome suggests one of two critical issues: 1) The BSTS synthetic control is highly unstable or sensitive to model specification, leading to an artificially low counterfactual prediction. 2) The DiD/PSM assumptions, while formally satisfied by diagnostics, mask underlying confounding, such as **heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE)** or **spatiotemporal spillovers**, which the current aggregate analysis fails to capture.

Future research must address this instability by employing methods that create a more robust counterfactual, such as **Synthetic Difference-in-Differences (SDID)**, and by decomposing the effect into sub-populations using **Causal Forest** methods to identify where the policy succeeded or failed.
