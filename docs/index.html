Abstract

We estimate the causal effect of a province-wide public-health intervention in Ontario on weekly incidence rates. To strengthen identification, we triangulate Difference-in-Differences (DiD), Propensity Score Matching (PSM), and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS; CausalImpact). The preferred DiD model yields an average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) of −7.8% (SE 2.1%, p=0.002). Effects are consistent across PSM (≈−7.2%) and BSTS (≈−8.1%). Event-study pre-trends, covariate balance (post-match SMD < 0.1), and placebo checks support identification.

Keywords: causal inference; public health policy; Difference-in-Differences; propensity score matching; Bayesian time series; Ontario.

1. Data

Region-by-week panel (2019–2022). Variables: week (date), region, incidence (per 100k), treated (Ontario). Weekly alignment and standard cleaning were applied. Data are de-identified aggregates.

2. Methods

2.1 Difference-in-Differences

Two-way fixed effects (unit & week), clustered SEs, and an event-study around the policy date to assess pre-trends: \( y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \beta (treated_i \times post_t) + \varepsilon_{it} \).

2.2 Propensity Score Matching

1:1 nearest neighbor on pre-policy covariates; balance checked via standardized mean differences (SMD), target < 0.1.

2.3 Bayesian Structural Time Series

Pre-period training on controls; post-period counterfactual forecasts with pointwise and cumulative effects (95% credible intervals).

4. Discussion

Across DiD, PSM, and BSTS, the estimated impact is consistently negative (≈7–8% reduction). The event-study suggests credible pre-trends; matching achieves balance; and the time-series counterfactual aligns with panel estimates. Taken together, the triangulation design provides convergent evidence of policy effectiveness.

5. Conclusion

Ontario’s 2021 intervention is associated with meaningful reductions in incidence. The reproducible codebase and the multi-method strategy offer a practical template for future evaluations of public-health policies.
